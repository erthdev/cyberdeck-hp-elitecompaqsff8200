# Log 01 — April 28, 2026

Today I finally started turning this old HP Compaq 8200 SFF into something more interesting than just another desktop sitting on a desk. The goal is to turn it into a cyberdeck-style machine, and this first log is basically the story of where the project began.

I tore the entire PC down and stripped it to its main parts. The whole point was to see what I was really working with, how much space I had, and whether the machine would still survive after a full disassembly. After putting it back together enough for a test, I powered it on and confirmed that it still worked.

That was not a guaranteed outcome, so getting it to boot again felt like a win.

The one problem I ran into was the CPU socket. When I tested it after the teardown, I got the three blinking red lights, which pointed to a CPU-related issue. I went back in, checked the seating, fixed the contact issue, and tried again. After that, the system came back to life properly. That was the first real troubleshooting moment of the build, and it taught me that this project is going to be a lot of careful reassembly, not just tearing things apart for the sake of it.

I also spent some time thinking about the power supply. At first, I tried to open it and see if I could make it smaller, but I quickly realized that idea was not worth pushing further. The PSU was too bulky, too awkward, and not really suited for the kind of compact build I want. So I dropped that plan and shifted to a Pico PSU setup instead.

That feels like the smarter route. It should be smaller, cleaner, and much more practical for a portable or cyberdeck-style build. I am also keeping the idea of reusing parts of the original PSU later on as a possible external power brick, but that is more of a future experiment than an immediate plan.

Right now, the important thing is simple: the machine still works, the motherboard is alive, the CPU issue has been fixed, and the project has a direction.

This first log is the starting point of the build. From here, I will document the Pico PSU conversion, adapter planning, and eventually the enclosure design that will turn this old desktop into something portable and unique.
